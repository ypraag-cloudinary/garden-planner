## Design Context

### Users
A dedicated care team that maintains a community garden with 27 physical rows in Israel. These are committed gardeners — not casual visitors — who coordinate planting, track what's growing where, and manage the garden season-to-season. They use the app both in the field (phone in hand, standing in the garden) and at home (planning the next planting cycle). Hebrew speakers, comfortable with mobile apps but not looking for complexity.

### Brand Personality
**Clean, quiet, trustworthy.** The app is a steady, reliable tool — not flashy, not playful, not corporate. It should feel like a well-designed modern farming instrument: purposeful, legible, grounded. Every screen should communicate "everything is under control" without visual noise.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Metaphor**: A modern agriculture app — functional, precise, connected to the land without being rustic or folksy.
- **Theme**: Light mode. Used outdoors in daylight and at home; light surfaces ensure readability in bright conditions.
- **Color**: Earth-toned greens and warm neutrals rooted in the garden context. Avoid saturated neons, purple/cyan tech palettes, and cold corporate grays. The palette should feel like it grew from soil — muted, natural, warm.
- **Typography**: Clean and legible, optimized for Hebrew RTL. Prioritize readability at arm's length on a phone screen. No decorative type.
- **Not this**: Not cute or cartoon-y (no emoji overload). Not a social media feed. Not a generic admin panel with tiny gray text and data tables. Not gamified.

### Design Principles
1. **Clarity over decoration.** Every element earns its place. If it doesn't help the gardener understand or act, remove it.
2. **Legible at a glance.** The app is used with dirty hands in bright sun. Text must be large enough, contrast high enough, and touch targets generous enough for field use.
3. **Calm authority.** The interface should feel like opening a well-organized notebook — everything has its place, nothing screams for attention.
4. **Grounded in the physical.** The 27 rows are real. The vegetables are real. The design should respect the physicality of the garden — proportional bars, spatial layout, earthy tones.
5. **Mobile-first, always.** Phone in the garden is the primary use case. Every design decision starts from a 375px screen held in one hand.
